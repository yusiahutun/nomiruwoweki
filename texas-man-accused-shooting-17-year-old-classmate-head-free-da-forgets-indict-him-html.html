A district attorney in Fort Bend County, Texas forgot to file some paperwork, and as a result, a man accused of murdering a his classmate will go free as early as today.

District Attorney John Healy takes ‘full responsibility’ for the slip-up and calls it ‘unfortunate’ that Richard Mendoza, Junior will be free on bail since no paperwork was filed in the 90 days following his arrest.

Police say Mendoza killed his friend and classmate, 17-year-old Christopher Daigle in November 2002 after catching Mr Daigle locking lips with his then-girlfriend.

Mr Healy apologised for his oversight, telling KTRK-TV News: ‘The bottom line is my office made a mistake and I accept full responsibility for it.’

The prosecutor was sanctioned, but wouldn’t go into details.

The family of the slain teen is far from pleased. The victim’s mother, Tracie Gregory, said something as serious as murder should be given utmost care by the courts.

Free to walk: Richard Mendoza, Junior posted a $75,000 bond

‘If it was their child, they wouldn’t have forgotten,’ she told KCEN-TV.

Mr Daigle’s aunt, Samantha Talley, said it was ‘absolute, total injustice’ that Mr Healy forgot to complete such an important step.

‘Fort Bend County said that they made a mistake – and it’s a big mistake – and they are letting a murderer walk free from this courtroom,’ she said.

Mr Daigle went missing in November 2002, days after Mendoza caught him kissing his girlfriend of two years.

It was only after years of investigation that detectives had a lead.

According to KTRK-TV News in Houston, investigators found human bones in a rural area in Fort Bend County. A handgun was also recovered.

Devastated: The victim's mother Tracie Gregory was sure if it were the DA's child, 'they wouldn't have forgotten'

Initial DNA tests showed they belonged to Mr Daigle.

Mendoza was arrested shortly after, in August of this year.

According to court records, police allege that Mendoza shot Mr Daigle in the back of the head with a 12-gauge shotgun in a field.

Lost boy: Christopher Daigle, 17, went missing in 2002. Police believe a body discovered in a field may be his

Mendoza had previously fabricated a story about he and Mr Daigle going to a nearby mall on the day he disappeared.

Because of the technicality, Judge Pedro Ruiz was forced to let Mendoza go on bond.

Though he set it high—at $75,000—though it’s a bond type that requires no down payment.

Terms of bond: Mendoza will be monitored by satellite at all times and will be arraigned in January

Ms Gregory is worried Mendoza, now 26, will run away, or attack the family.

Mendoza’s lawyer, however, vows that he’s neither a flight risk nor a threat.

Piecing together: After initial DNA tests, detectives believe the bones found in a rural field to be those of Mr Daigle

Mendoza is to be set up with a satellite monitoring system that will keep tabs on his location at all times. It may be ready as early as today.

Under the conditions of posting bond, Mendoza is forbidden to have any contact with Daigle’s family.
